DNA and Protein Synthesis

Transcription

Transcription is when mRNA is created based on a section of DNA in the nucleus of the cell. The DNA is unzipped, and one of the strands becomes the coding strand. RNA polymerase creates RNA by pairing the correct bases withe the pre-existing ones, only with uracil instead of thymine. Then, the mRNA leaves the nucleus.

Translation

Translation is when the rRNA reads the mRNA and translates it into amino acids based on the codons (sections of three bases.) The rRNA does not start translating the bases until it has read the start codon. There is also a stop codon, which tells the rRNA when to stop translating. Replication

DNA Structure

DNA is made up of deoxyribose sugars, phosphate groups, and nitrogenous bases. the bases are held together with hydrogen bonds, and the phosphates and sugars are connected by covalent bonds. DNA has an easily recognizable double-helix shape, with the bases on the inside. One of the most important parts of DNA structure is the way that bases pair together, called the base-pairing rule. The base-pairing rule states that only adenine and thymine can pair together, as well as cytosine and guanine.

RNA is used to create proteins, and is fairly similar to DNA in some aspects. However, there are some key differences.

  • RNA is single-stranded
  • RNA is made with ribose sugars, not deoxyribose sugars
  • RNA cannot contain thymine, so instead, uracil pairs with adenine in its place
There are three different types of RNA: mRNA, rRNA, and tRNA. mRNA is the copy of a section of DNA used as a blueprint for protein creation. rRNA makes up the ribosome, where mRNA is read and translated. tRNA brings amino acids to connect to the polymer that the rRNA decodes.
